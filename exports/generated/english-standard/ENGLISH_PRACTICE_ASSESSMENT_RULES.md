# English Practice Rules

## Practice Overview

These rules define how the `english` domain pack maps framework practice types to English for Software Engineers. They support future lessons, speaking practice, listening practice, shadowing, writing review, interview simulations, portfolio work, review sessions, and assessments.

This file does not create full exercises, dialogues, listening transcripts, shadowing scripts, lesson bodies, learner state, schemas, or executable tooling.

Practice output SHOULD follow `templates/session/PRACTICE_OUTPUT_TEMPLATE.md`.

## Practice Principles

- Practice SHOULD target learning skills from `domains/english/SKILL_GRAPH.md`.
- Practice SHOULD follow `core/learning-engine/PRACTICE_MODEL.md`.
- Domain rules MUST NOT weaken `core/mastery-model/EVIDENCE_REQUIREMENTS.md`.
- Learner progress belongs in learner state, not this domain pack.
- Practice output MAY propose learner state updates but MUST NOT silently apply them.

- English target examples, phrases, collocations, interview answers, and learner outputs SHOULD remain in English where appropriate.

## Practice Type Mapping

### Recall Practice

- Purpose: Strengthen retrieval of grammar rules, vocabulary, collocations, phrase patterns, pronunciation distinctions, and interview structures.
- Suitable learning skills or stages: Stage 2 grammar, Stage 4 vocabulary, Stage 5 pronunciation awareness, Stage 9 interview structures.
- Expected learner output: short answer, definition, example sentence, collocation choice, or phrase substitution.
- Evidence value: supports M1 or M2 and may support M3 when output is produced without seeing the answer first.
- Common failure patterns: recognition without production, memorized definitions without use, wrong phrase context.

### Guided Practice

- Purpose: Build controlled production with prompts, scaffolding, models, or partial sentence structures.
- Suitable learning skills or stages: Stage 1 confidence, Stage 2 grammar, Stage 3 status updates, Stage 6 technical explanation.
- Expected learner output: guided spoken answer, controlled written response, corrected sentence, or scaffolded explanation.
- Evidence value: supports M3 when feedback identifies what the learner can do with support.
- Common failure patterns: repeating model text without adaptation, overreliance on prompts, incomplete self-correction.

### Independent Practice

- Purpose: Test learner production with minimal support.
- Suitable learning skills or stages: Stage 3 work updates, Stage 6 technical explanation, Stage 8 writing, Stage 10 long-form speaking.
- Expected learner output: spoken response, written message, technical explanation, listening summary, or interview answer.
- Evidence value: supports M4 when reviewed for correctness, independence, clarity, and task fit.
- Common failure patterns: grammar collapse under independence, vague output, direct translation, weak structure.

### Mixed Practice

- Purpose: Combine grammar, vocabulary, listening, speaking, writing, workplace context, and review.
- Suitable learning skills or stages: Stage 5 listening plus speaking, Stage 7 meetings, Stage 8 writing, Stage 10 fluency and nuance.
- Expected learner output: role-play summary, meeting response, blocker explanation, revised written artifact, or combined listening/speaking response.
- Evidence value: supports transfer when multiple learning skills are demonstrated together.
- Common failure patterns: success in one mode only, weak transfer, missing evidence for one component.

### Error-Based Practice

- Purpose: Repair observed grammar, pronunciation, phrasing, vocabulary, clarity, tone, listening, or structure mistakes.
- Suitable learning skills or stages: all stages, especially review and maintenance.
- Expected learner output: corrected sentence, revised message, repeated spoken answer, pronunciation repair, or self-correction note.
- Evidence value: supports evidence for weak-area repair when the correction is reviewed.
- Common failure patterns: correcting only surface form, introducing new errors, treating one correction as mastery.

### Project-Based Practice

- Purpose: Build speaking portfolio, interview portfolio, workplace communication portfolio, technical explanation portfolio, shadowing log, or writing portfolio artifacts.
- Suitable learning skills or stages: Stage 11 portfolio work and cross-cutting transfer learning skills.
- Expected learner output: reviewed artifact, recording summary, writing sample, interview response set, or technical explanation portfolio item.
- Evidence value: may support M5 when reviewed and connected to realistic communication contexts.
- Common failure patterns: artifact without review, artifact not mapped to learning skills, no reflection or limitation notes.

### Interview-Style Practice

- Purpose: Practice follow-up questions, technical explanations, behavioral answers, clarification, unknown questions, and pressure handling.
- Suitable learning skills or stages: Stage 9 interview English and Stage 10 explanation defense.
- Expected learner output: interview answer, follow-up response, clarification question, tradeoff defense, or self-corrected answer.
- Evidence value: may support M5 or M6 when responses are captured or summarized and evaluated.
- Common failure patterns: memorized answers, fluent but inaccurate responses, weak follow-up, pretending to know.

## Practice Difficulty Guidance

- D1: Recognition and controlled recall; learner identifies terms, grammar patterns, phrases, or communication structures.
- D2: Guided sentence or phrase production; learner produces controlled output with prompts or models.
- D3: Independent short output; learner produces short spoken or written output with minimal support.
- D4: Integrated workplace or technical communication; learner combines language skills in realistic contexts.
- D5: Portfolio, interview, or pressure-based defense; learner produces or defends output under review, follow-up, or realistic constraints.

Difficulty labels are practice guidance, not mastery records.

## Evidence-Producing Practice

Learner output can support evidence when it includes one or more of:

- Spoken answer captured or summarized.
- Written answer or revised message.
- Grammar correction before-and-after.
- Vocabulary or collocation used in context.
- Listening summary or detail extraction.
- Shadowing attempt with feedback.
- Pronunciation review note.
- Technical explanation.
- Meeting role-play summary.
- Interview answer with follow-up.
- Portfolio artifact.

Evidence SHOULD identify the learner output, target learning skill, feedback or review, limitations, and possible state impact.

Practice participation, passive listening, repeated shadowing, reading generated dialogues, or lesson completion alone does not imply mastery.

## Speaking Practice Rules

- Short answers SHOULD target confidence, accuracy, and relevant vocabulary before speed.
- Structured answers SHOULD include context, main point, support, and closing or next step.
- Long-form speaking SHOULD preserve coherence, grammar accuracy, and audience awareness.
- Follow-up questions SHOULD test whether the learner can clarify, extend, or defend an answer.
- Hesitation and filler control SHOULD improve communication without forcing unnatural speed.
- Fluency and accuracy SHOULD be balanced; neither SHOULD erase the other.
- Captured or summarized spoken output is required before speaking practice can support evidence.

## Grammar Accuracy Practice Rules

- Grammar practice SHOULD support communication, not become isolated grammar-only learning.
- Grammar in speaking SHOULD be practiced through short answers, work updates, explanations, and interview responses.
- Grammar in writing SHOULD be practiced through emails, chat messages, PR comments, summaries, and revisions.
- Error patterns SHOULD be tracked conceptually for targeted practice.
- Self-correction SHOULD include the original error, corrected output, and review.
- Articles, prepositions, tense, aspect, modals, and conditionals SHOULD be practiced in workplace or technical contexts.
- Grammar practice SHOULD connect to `english.sentence-structure-error-correction`, `english.grammar-speaking-integration`, and related learning skills.

## Listening Practice Rules

- Main idea practice SHOULD ask the learner to summarize overall meaning.
- Detail extraction SHOULD ask for facts, decisions, constraints, action items, or speaker intent.
- Workplace discussion practice SHOULD focus on blockers, decisions, owners, and next steps.
- Technical explanation listening SHOULD focus on concepts, examples, cause/effect, and tradeoffs.
- Learner summaries SHOULD be compared to source content when available.
- Listening summaries, answers, or action-item notes MAY become evidence when reviewed.
- Listening exposure alone does not imply mastery.

## Shadowing and Pronunciation Practice Rules

- Shadowing SHOULD improve rhythm, automatic phrasing, listening-to-speech connection, and pronunciation awareness.
- Pronunciation practice SHOULD prioritize intelligibility and clarity over accent imitation.
- Word stress and sentence stress SHOULD be reviewed when they affect meaning or listener comprehension.
- Intonation practice SHOULD support professional clarity, politeness, and coherence.
- Connected speech recognition SHOULD help the learner understand natural speech without requiring imitation of every accent feature.
- Shadowing attempts MAY support evidence only when captured or summarized and reviewed.
- Repetition without feedback MUST NOT imply mastery.

## Writing Practice Rules

- Email practice SHOULD focus on subject, context, purpose, request, tone, and closing.
- Chat message practice SHOULD focus on brevity, context, actionability, and professional tone.
- PR comment practice SHOULD focus on specificity, rationale, and respectful feedback.
- Technical summary practice SHOULD focus on accuracy, structure, audience fit, and concision.
- Documentation note practice SHOULD focus on clarity, assumptions, steps, and reader needs.
- Tone adjustment SHOULD consider audience, channel, urgency, and relationship.
- Before-and-after revision evidence SHOULD be used when writing practice supports progress or mastery recommendations.

## Error-Based Practice Rules

Common English error categories SHOULD map to targeted repair:

- Tense choice error: review `english.present-simple-continuous` or `english.past-simple-present-perfect`.
- Article or determiner error: review `english.articles-determiners`.
- Preposition error: review `english.prepositions-work-communication`.
- Word order error: review `english.sentence-structure-error-correction`.
- Direct translation: review `english.avoiding-direct-translation`.
- Unnatural collocation: review `english.collocations`.
- Unclear reference: review `english.professional-writing` or `english.decision-summary-confirmation`.
- Overly direct tone: review `english.professional-tone-filler-linking`.
- Filler overuse: review `english.reducing-hesitation` or `english.fluency-accuracy-pressure`.
- Pronunciation clarity issue: review `english.pronunciation-clarity-stress`.
- Weak answer structure: review `english.behavioral-interview-answers` or `english.technical-interview-answers`.
- Vague technical explanation: review `english.technical-concept-explanation`.
- Missing example: review `english.examples-simplification`.
- Weak follow-up answer: review `english.handling-followups-unknowns`.
- Listening gist or detail mismatch: review `english.listening-main-idea` or `english.listening-detail`.

- Assessment SHOULD target learning skills from `domains/english/SKILL_GRAPH.md`.

## Core Dimension Mapping

### Correctness

- Meaning in English for Software Engineers: grammar, vocabulary, pronunciation, meaning, and task fit are accurate enough for the communication context.
- Evidence examples: reviewed spoken answer, written message, corrected sentence, listening summary, technical explanation, or interview response.
- Warning signs: meaning changes, repeated grammar errors, unclear pronunciation, wrong vocabulary, or answer does not address the prompt.

### Independence

- Meaning in English for Software Engineers: learner can speak, write, listen, or respond without excessive prompting, translation, or scripted dependence.
- Evidence examples: free speaking response, independent written artifact, answer after minimal prompt, or self-managed clarification.
- Warning signs: cannot continue without heavy hints, only repeats generated text, or depends on the instruction language for every step.

### Explanation Quality

- Meaning in English for Software Engineers: communication is clear, structured, relevant, detailed enough, and adapted to audience.
- Evidence examples: technical explanation, interview answer, blocker explanation, decision summary, or meeting contribution.
- Warning signs: unclear structure, missing context, too little detail, memorized phrasing without meaning, or no example.

### Transfer Ability

- Meaning in English for Software Engineers: learner uses English in new workplace, technical, interview, listening, writing, or portfolio contexts.
- Evidence examples: adapting an answer to a new question, explaining a new concept, revising tone for audience, or transferring feedback to a new artifact.
- Warning signs: performance works only for rehearsed scripts, familiar examples, or one communication mode.

### Consistency

- Meaning in English for Software Engineers: learner performs reliably across repeated attempts and reduces recurring mistakes.
- Evidence examples: multiple reviewed outputs showing stable grammar, clarity, pronunciation, vocabulary use, or interaction ability.
- Warning signs: repeated same tense error, recurring direct translation, inconsistent pronunciation clarity, or unstable follow-up responses.

### Speed

- Meaning in English for Software Engineers: response time and fluency matter only when the context requires conversation, interviews, or time-sensitive communication.
- Evidence examples: timely answer with acceptable accuracy, smooth follow-up response, reduced hesitation, or clear status update under pressure.
- Warning signs: fast but inaccurate speech, fluent memorized answers, or speed used to ignore clarity and correctness.

## English-Specific Assessment Areas

- Fluency: flow, hesitation control, and ability to continue communication.
- Accuracy: grammar, vocabulary, pronunciation, and meaning correctness.
- Pronunciation clarity: intelligibility, stress, rhythm, and listener comprehension where relevant.
- Vocabulary activation: ability to use known words, phrases, and collocations in context.
- Natural phrasing: reduced direct translation and more idiomatic professional English.
- Listening comprehension: main idea, details, speaker intent, decisions, and action items.
- Interaction ability: asking, clarifying, responding, interrupting politely, and handling follow-ups.
- Professional tone: channel-appropriate, respectful, clear, and balanced communication.
- Technical explanation quality: structure, correctness, examples, tradeoffs, and audience fit.
- Interview readiness: structured answers, follow-up handling, uncertainty management, and pressure control.
- Writing clarity: concision, task fit, grammar, tone, and revision quality.

## Mastery-Level Expectations

- M0: No reliable evidence is available.
- M1: Learner recognizes words, phrases, grammar patterns, pronunciation features, or communication structures.
- M2: Learner explains language use or identifies correct patterns.
- M3: Learner produces controlled output with guidance, prompts, or scaffolding.
- M4: Learner produces independent spoken, written, listening-summary, or interaction output in familiar contexts.
- M5: Learner transfers English to workplace, technical, interview, portfolio, or realistic communication contexts.
- M6: Learner explains and defends ideas under follow-up questioning with clarity, accuracy, and consistency.

These are assessment expectations, not learner state records.

## Evidence Requirements by Work Type

- Speaking evidence: captured or summarized spoken response with review of fluency, accuracy, clarity, and task fit.
- Grammar evidence: original output, correction or revised output, and review of the target grammar pattern.
- Vocabulary/collocation evidence: learner output using terms or collocations accurately in context.
- Listening evidence: learner summary, detail extraction, action-item notes, or answers compared to source content.
- Shadowing/pronunciation evidence: captured or summarized attempt with feedback on intelligibility, stress, rhythm, or clarity.
- Writing evidence: written artifact, revision, and review of clarity, tone, grammar, and task fit.
- Technical explanation evidence: reviewed explanation with context, structure, terminology, example, and follow-up if available.
- Meeting/role-play evidence: summarized interaction showing questions, responses, clarification, and professional tone.
- Interview response evidence: captured or summarized answer with follow-up evaluation.
- Portfolio artifact evidence: reviewed artifact connected to learning skills, feedback, limitations, and transfer context.

## Speaking Assessment Guidance

- Speaking assessment SHOULD use captured or summarized learner output.
- Assessment SHOULD evaluate fluency, accuracy, pronunciation clarity, structure, and task fit.
- Short answers SHOULD not be over-penalized for limited complexity when the target is foundation-level output.
- Long-form speaking SHOULD be assessed for coherence, accuracy retention, and audience awareness.
- Speaking participation alone MUST NOT imply mastery.

## Listening and Pronunciation Assessment Guidance

- Listening assessment SHOULD compare learner summaries or answers to source content when available.
- Listening exposure alone MUST NOT imply mastery.
- Pronunciation assessment SHOULD prioritize intelligibility and clarity over accent imitation.
- Shadowing repetition alone MUST NOT imply mastery.
- Pronunciation evidence SHOULD note the reviewed feature, such as clarity, word stress, sentence stress, intonation, or connected speech recognition.

## Writing Assessment Guidance

- Writing assessment SHOULD review clarity, grammar, tone, audience fit, actionability, and concision.
- Before-and-after revisions SHOULD be used when assessing self-correction or improvement.
- Emails, chat messages, PR comments, technical summaries, and documentation notes SHOULD be assessed against their communication purpose.
- Writing polish MUST NOT hide missing meaning or unclear task fit.

## Interview Assessment Guidance

- Interview assessment SHOULD include follow-up questions when possible.
- Behavioral answers SHOULD be assessed for concrete context, action, result, reflection, and relevance.
- Technical answers SHOULD be assessed for correctness, structure, explanation quality, and handling of uncertainty.
- Speed MAY matter when the goal is interview fluency, but correctness and clarity remain primary.
- Fluent wording MUST NOT be treated as correctness.
